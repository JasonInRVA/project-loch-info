What this site is

This site collects and explains public records concerning Project Loch. It is intended to help residents understand the proposal and participate in an informed discussion. It does not take a position for or against the project.

The core public record currently consists of a U.S. Army Corps of Engineers public notice, a three-volume wetlands application package, Virginia agency correspondence, and later applicant responses. Some parts of the proposal have evolved since the original application, so no single document should be treated as the complete current picture.

What appears to have changed

The mitigation plan evolved after the original notice. The original federal notice described a permittee-responsible mitigation site in Cumberland County known as the Wauford site. Later filings indicate a move toward purchasing wetland and stream mitigation credits instead. Readers should compare the original notice with the later VMRC/DEQ docket materials rather than assuming the May notice reflects the final mitigation approach.

Agency correspondence also indicates continuing review of alternatives, hydrology, avoidance and minimization, mitigation availability, and the documentation supporting project design decisions.

Regulatory timeline

  1. January–May 2026: Engineering drawings and wetland-impact maps were revised multiple times.
  2. May 13, 2026: USACE published the federal Section 404 public notice.
  3. June 11, 2026: The federal public-comment period listed in that notice closed.
  4. June–July 2026: DEQ correspondence and applicant response packages continued to address additional information and mitigation.
  5. Current status: Final federal and state permit decisions were not identified in the public materials reviewed for this site as of July 10, 2026.

Questions worth asking Google

Design and alternatives

  • Which elements of the current site layout are still changeable?
  • Why are each of the remaining wetland and stream impacts unavoidable?
  • What alternatives were considered and rejected, and on what criteria?

Water and ecology

  • How will pre- and post-development hydrology compare?
  • What monitoring will detect downstream changes after construction?
  • Why did the mitigation approach change, and what local environmental benefit was lost or gained?

Operations

  • What are the expected water, electricity, and backup-generator requirements?
  • What enforceable limits will apply to noise, lighting, emissions, and generator testing?
  • Will operational data and compliance reporting be made public?

Community commitments

  • What road, emergency-response, conservation, or community investments will Google fund?
  • Which commitments will be legally enforceable rather than voluntary?
  • How many permanent local jobs are expected, and what kinds?

Important context

Public permit records are snapshots of an evolving proposal. Agency questions do not necessarily mean a permit will be denied, and applicant assertions do not necessarily mean an issue has been resolved. The strongest conclusions come from comparing the original application, agency comments, applicant responses, and final permit conditions when those become available.
